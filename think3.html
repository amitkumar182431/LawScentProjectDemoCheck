Judicial Independence

By Supreme Court Judge

Judicial independence is a cornerstone of India's constitutional democracy. It ensures that judges can decide cases impartially, free from external pressure or influence. The principle flows directly from the doctrine of separation of powers, embedded within the Constitution of India (26 January 1950).

Constitutional Framework

Articles 124 and 217 of the Constitution safeguard the independence of the Supreme Court and High Courts by providing security of tenure, fixed service conditions, and protection from arbitrary removal. Judges can only be removed through impeachment by Parliament under Article 124(4).

Landmark Judgments & Judicial Accountability

In Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973), the Supreme Court held that judicial independence is part of the Basic Structure of the Constitution, which cannot be altered even by constitutional amendments.

The Second Judges Case (Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India, 1993) and the Third Judges Case (1998) reinforced the primacy of the judiciary in judicial appointments through the collegium system, strengthening institutional autonomy.

While independence protects judges from interference, accountability is ensured through judicial discipline, transparency in proceedings, and reasoned judgments, maintaining public confidence in the justice system.
